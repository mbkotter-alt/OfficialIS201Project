The ACT Is a Clock Problem

Why time management beats content knowledge for most students

Posted on June 17, 2026

Almost every student I've tutored comes in assuming the ACT is a knowledge test — that if they just review enough algebra and grammar rules, the score will follow. After a 36 and years of coaching students through the exam, I'd put it differently: the ACT is a clock problem wearing a content test's clothes.

Here's what I mean. The math section gives you 60 questions in 60 minutes. That's a minute a question on paper, but the questions aren't evenly difficult — the back third of the section can easily eat three or four minutes each if you try to solve them the "proper" way. Students who know the material cold still run out of time, not because they couldn't solve question 58, but because they spent four minutes solving question 12 when forty-five seconds would've done it.

Where Students Actually Lose Time

Three patterns show up over and over with students I coach:

Re-reading instead of annotating. On the reading section especially, students re-read whole passages looking for an answer instead of marking key details the first time through. That single habit change — underline as you go, don't reread — recovers more time than any amount of vocabulary study.

Solving instead of estimating. A lot of ACT math questions are built so that a rough estimate eliminates three of the five answer choices in seconds. Full algebraic solutions are often slower than they need to be when the answer choices themselves are doing half the work for you.

Treating every question as equally worth fighting for. A hard question is worth exactly as many points as an easy one. Students who refuse to skip and come back lose minutes defending their pride on one question instead of banking points on three easier ones.

What This Means in Practice

When I sit down with a student, the first thing we build isn't a content review schedule — it's a pacing plan. We time individual sections, not just full tests, so a student knows exactly where their personal slowdown points are. Most students discover their content knowledge was never really the problem.

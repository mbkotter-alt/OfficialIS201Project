Notes From an ACT Math Tutor

I scored a 36 on the ACT and have spent the years since tutoring students through the same test. This page is a scratch space for the patterns I actually use when a student is stuck — not a polished course, just the notes I'd hand a tutee.

How I Break Down a Hard Question

Most ACT Math questions that feel impossible are really two or three easier questions stacked together. Here's the order I walk through with a student, and the categories that order tends to cover:

  1. Identify what's actually being asked
    • Underline the final question, not the setup
    • Note what units or form the answer needs to be in
  2. Match it to a known category
    • Algebra & functions
    • Geometry & trigonometry
    • Probability & statistics
  3. Pick the fastest legal shortcut
    • Plug in answer choices when solving directly is slower
    • Estimate first to eliminate obviously wrong choices

Two Questions Worth Trying Yourself

Cover the answer, time yourself, then check your work against the strategy below.

  1. Algebra. If 3(x − 4) = 2x + 5, what is the value of x?
    • Distribute the left side first: 3x − 12 = 2x + 5
    • Move variables to one side: x = 17
  2. Geometry. A rectangle has a perimeter of 36 and a length twice its width. What is its area?
    • Let width = w, length = 2w. Perimeter: 2(w + 2w) = 36, so 3w = 18, w = 6
    • Length = 12, so area = 6 × 12 = 72
